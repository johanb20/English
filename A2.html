Relative Clauses of Time
Date When
Mother's Day is a day when my kids make pancakes for me.
February 14th is the day when people give cards to the ones they love.
New Year's Eve is a night when I have fun with my friends.

Adverbial Clauses of Time
Adverbial Clause
When women get married, they usually wear a brightly colored sari.
After the groom arrives, the bride and groom exchange garlands of flowers.
Before the wedding, the bride's female relatives usually have a party to celebrate.

When: Se usa para hablar de un momento general o específico en el que algo ocurre.
When marca el momento en que algo sucede.

After: Se usa para indicar que una acción ocurre DESPUÉS de otra.
After = algo pasa después de otra cosa.

Before: Se usa para indicar que una acción ocurre ANTES de otra.
Before = algo pasa antes de otra cosa.

Time contrasts
Past Present Future
A few years ago, there were just houses here. These days, they're building lots of apartments. Soon, there will be apartment blocks everywhere.
We used to go to a burger place after class every day. Today, people order food from their phones. In the future, restaurants might not exist.

Future forms and expressions: meaning and usage
Word / Expression Traslate Usage
Will Futuro simple (hará, será, tendrá) Se usa para hablar de decisiones espontáneas, promesas y consecuencias seguras en el futuro
May Puede que / tal vez Se usa para expresar posibilidad en el futuro, cuando no se está seguro de que algo ocurra
Might Podría / quizá Se usa para indicar una posibilidad más baja o menos segura que “may”
Have Tener Se usa para expresar posesión o situaciones futuras cuando se combina con “will”
Be able to Poder / ser capaz de Se usa para expresar habilidad o capacidad en el presente o futuro, especialmente cuando “can” no es posible
Won’t No hará / no será / no tendrá Forma negativa de “will”; se usa para negar acciones futuras o expresar rechazo

Conditional sentences with if-clauses
If the possible situation comes first, it must be separated from the consequence with a comma. However, if the consequence comes first, the comma is no longer needed.
Possible situation (present) Consequence (future with will, may, or might) Consequence (Spanish translation)
If you get a high-paying job, you 'll have more cash to spend. tendrás más dinero para gastar.
If you have more cash to spend, you 'll be able to buy anything you want. podrás comprar lo que quieras.
If you can buy anything you want, you won't save your money. no ahorrarás tu dinero.
If you don't save your money, you may have to get a weekend job. puede que tengas que conseguir un trabajo de fin de semana.
If you have to get a weekend job, you might not have any free time. quizá no tengas tiempo libre.

Passive with by (Simple Past)
The passive changes the focus of a sentence.
For the simple past, use: past of be + past participle.
Active Passive
The Disney brothers founded the company in 1923. It was founded by the Disney brothers in 1923.
Walt Disney opened Disneyland in 1955. Disneyland was opened by Walt Disney in 1955.
The ABC network broadcast the opening of the park. The opening was broadcast by ABC.

Grammar

  • Past continuous vs. simple past → Pasado continuo vs. pasado simple
  • Present perfect continuous → Presente perfecto continuo

Past Continuous vs. Simple Past
Use the past continuous for an action in progress in the past.
Use the simple past for an action that interrupts it.
Past Continuous (Action in Progress) Simple Past (Interrupting Action)
I was having lunch when I spilled coffee on my clothes.
I was driving to the airport, but I got a flat tire.
While I was shopping one day, a celebrity walked into the store.

Present Perfect Continuous
Use the present perfect continuous for actions that start in the past and continue into the present.
Questions Answers
What have you been doing lately? I've been working two jobs for the last six months.
How long have you been trying? I've been trying since I graduated.
Have you been saving money? No, I haven't been saving money. I've been spending it!
